Background
4
The RCOG published a safety alert on Parvovirus B19 following a potentially avoidable death of a baby in 2012.
5
In children typically presents with characteristic diffuse erythematous facial rash - 'slapped cheek' following around 5 days of prodromal symptoms
6
In adults symptoms often atypical or asymptomatic in up to 50% of cases
7
Infectivity period is 7-10 days before the rash develops to 1 day after rash onset
8
Incubation period is typically around 7 days (range 4-14 days)
9
10
Investigations
11
In healthy children and adults diagnosis is usually clinical and no lab confirmation is required.
12
If a pregnant woman has had exposure to Parvovirus or is suspected of having Parvovirus then serological tests are indicated.
13
NICE advise contacting the local virology/microbiology/infectious disease unit for clarification of tests but they are likely to include Parvovirus B19-specific IgG and IgM and may need viral PCR.
14
Note Rubella should also be tested for as can cause similar symptoms
