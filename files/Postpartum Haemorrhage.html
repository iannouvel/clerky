Management
19
If known risk factors - should deliver in hospital with access to transfusion
20
Minor PPH - intravenous access (one 14-gauge cannula) & urgent venepuncture (20 ml) for: G&S, FBC, Coag and fibrinogen, & obs (HR / RR / 15 min BP) & warmed crystalloid infusion
21
Major PPH - in addition to minor PPH measurs, ensure airway / breathing / circulation assessed serially, flatten patient / bed, keep war,, transfuse ASAP, infuse warmed crystalloid
22
If needed, transfuse O- K- blood until appropriately matched blood available.
23
If blood needed and atypical antibodies present, discuss closely with transfusion / haematology
24
Consider use of cell salvage if high risk PPH (both at C-section and vaginal delivery)
25
If ongoing bleeding and concerned regarding amount lost, consider 4 units RBC and 12-15ml/kg FFP
26
If suspected coagulopathy (abruption / AFE / delayed recognition of PPH) consider FFP early
27
If PT or APTT > 1.5 normal and ongoing bleeding, likely to need > 12-15 ml / kg FFP
28
Maintain fibrinogen > 2, if <2 give cryoprecipitate
29
If ongoing PPH and platelets < 75, give platelets

Management of failed pharmacological mx of PPH
51
If pharmacological measures fail to control the haemorrhage, surgical interventions should be initiated sooner rather than later.
52
Intrauterine balloon tamponade is an appropriate first-line ‘surgical’ intervention for most women where uterine atony is the only or main cause of haemorrhage.
53
Conservative surgical interventions may be attempted as second line, depending on clinical circumstances and available expertise.
54
It is recommended that a laminated diagram of the brace suture technique be kept in theatre.
55
Resort to hysterectomy sooner rather than later (especially in cases of placenta accreta or uterine rupture).
56
Ideally and when feasible, a second experienced clinician should be involved in the decision for hysterectomy
