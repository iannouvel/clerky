Key Points
4
Lactation/Galactopoiesis is maintained via the action of Prolactin
5
Lactation will usually stop naturally within 7 days when suckling ceases
6
Lactation may be suppressed by using drugs that inhibit prolactin but these are not without side effects.
7
Cabergoline and Bromocriptine and the most commonly used drugs for lactation suppression
8
9
Reasons for pharmacological suppression of lactation
10
There are a number of reasons mothers may wish to stop lactation:
11
Stillbirth/adoption
12
Maternal infection eg HIV (especially if not on retroviral therapy)
13
Prolonged lactation after stopping breastfeeding (other causes hyperprolactinaemia should be excluded)
14
15
Cabergoline is contraindicated in the following:
16
Pre-eclampsia
17
Cardiac valvulopathy (exclude before treatment)
18
History of fibrotic disorders (eg pericardial / pulmonary / retroperitoneal)
19
History of puerperal psychosis
20
Cabergoline should also not be used in patients with hypersensitivity to ergot alkaloids as there is a high risk of cross reactivity.
