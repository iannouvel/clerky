1.2 Gestational diabetes
183
Risk assessment, testing and diagnosis
184
Risk assessment
185
1.2.1To help women make an informed decision about risk assessment and testing for gestational diabetes, explain that:
186
187
some women find that gestational diabetes can be controlled with changes in diet and exercise
188
most women with gestational diabetes will need oral blood glucose‑lowering agents or insulin
189
if gestational diabetes is not detected and controlled, there is a small increase in the risk of serious adverse birth complications such as shoulder dystocia
190
women with gestational diabetes will need more monitoring, and may need more interventions during pregnancy and labour. [2015]
191
1.2.2Assess the risk of gestational diabetes using risk factors in a healthy population. At the booking appointment, check for the following risk factors:
192
193
BMI above 30 kg/m2
194
previous macrosomic baby weighing 4.5 kg or more
195
previous gestational diabetes
196
family history of diabetes (first‑degree relative with diabetes)
197
an ethnicity with a high prevalence of diabetes.
198
199
Offer women with any of these risk factors testing for gestational diabetes (see recommendations 1.2.5 to 1.2.7). [2008, amended 2015]
200
1.2.3Do not use fasting plasma glucose, random blood glucose, HbA1c, glucose challenge test or urinalysis for glucose to assess the risk of developing gestational diabetes. [2015]
201
Glycosuria detected by routine antenatal testing
202
1.2.4Consider further testing to exclude gestational diabetes in women who have the following reagent strip test results during routine antenatal care:
203
204
glycosuria of 2+ or above on 1 occasion
205
glycosuria of 1+ or above on 2 or more occasions. [2015]
206
Testing
207
1.2.5Use the 75-g 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) to test for gestational diabetes in women with risk factors (see recommendation 1.2.2). [2015]
208
1.2.6For women who have had gestational diabetes in a previous pregnancy, offer:
209
210
early self‑monitoring of blood glucose or
211
a 75-g 2‑hour OGTT as soon as possible after booking (whether in the first or second trimester), and a further 75-g 2‑hour OGTT at 24 to 28 weeks if the results of the first OGTT are normal. [2015]
212
1.2.7Offer women with any of the other risk factors for gestational diabetes (see recommendation 1.2.2) a 75-g 2‑hour OGTT at 24 to 28 weeks. [2015]
213
Diagnosis
214
1.2.8Diagnose gestational diabetes if the woman has either:
215
216
a fasting plasma glucose level of 5.6 mmol/litre or above or
217
a 2‑hour plasma glucose level of 7.8 mmol/litre or above. [2015]
218
1.2.9When women are diagnosed with gestational diabetes:
219
220
offer a review with the joint diabetes and antenatal clinic within 1 week.
221
tell their primary healthcare team (see also the section on continuity of care in the NICE guideline on patient experience in adult NHS services). [2015]

Interventions
223
1.2.10Explain to women with gestational diabetes:
224
225
the implications (both short and long term) of the diagnosis for her and her baby (including UK government advice on driving with diabetes)
226
that good blood glucose control throughout pregnancy will reduce the risk of fetal macrosomia, trauma during birth (for her and her baby), induction of labour and/or caesarean section, neonatal hypoglycaemia, and perinatal death
227
that treatment includes changes in diet and exercise, and could involve medicines. [2015]
228
1.2.11Teach women with gestational diabetes how to self‑monitor their blood glucose. [2015]
229
1.2.12Use the same capillary plasma glucose target levels for women with gestational diabetes as for women with pre‑existing diabetes (see recommendations 1.3.5 and 1.3.6). [2015]
230
1.2.13Tailor blood glucose‑lowering therapy to the blood glucose profile and personal preferences of the woman with gestational diabetes. [2015]
231
1.2.14When women are diagnosed with gestational diabetes, offer advice about changes in diet and exercise. [2015]
232
1.2.15Advise women with gestational diabetes to eat a healthy diet during pregnancy, and to switch from high to low glycaemic index food. [2015]
233
1.2.16Refer all women with gestational diabetes to a dietitian. [2015]
234
1.2.17Advise women with gestational diabetes to exercise regularly (for example, walking for 30 minutes after a meal). [2015]
235
1.2.18For women with gestational diabetes who have a fasting plasma glucose level below 7 mmol/litre at diagnosis, offer a trial of diet and exercise changes. [2015]
236
1.2.19If blood glucose targets are not met with diet and exercise changes within 1 to 2 weeks, offer metformin.
237
238
Note that this is an off-label use. See NICE's information on prescribing medicines. [2015]
239
1.2.20If metformin is contraindicated or unacceptable to the woman, offer insulin. [2015]
240
1.2.21If blood glucose targets are not met with diet and exercise changes plus metformin, offer insulin as well.
241
242
Note that this is an off-label use of metformin. See NICE's information on prescribing medicines. [2015]
243
1.2.22For women with gestational diabetes who have a fasting plasma glucose level of 7.0 mmol/litre or above at diagnosis, offer:
244
245
immediate treatment with insulin, with or without metformin and
246
diet and exercise changes.
247
248
Note that this is an off-label use of metformin. See NICE's information on prescribing medicines. [2015]
249
1.2.23For women with gestational diabetes who have a fasting plasma glucose level of between 6.0 and 6.9 mmol/litre and complications such as macrosomia or hydramnios, consider:
250
251
immediate treatment with insulin, with or without metformin and
252
diet and exercise changes.
253
254
Note that this is an off-label use of metformin. See NICE's information on prescribing medicines. [2015]

1.6 Postnatal care
407
Blood glucose control, medicines and breastfeeding
408
1.6.1Women with insulin‑treated pre‑existing diabetes should reduce their insulin immediately after birth and monitor their blood glucose levels to find the appropriate dose. [2008]
409
1.6.2Explain to women with insulin‑treated pre‑existing diabetes that they are at increased risk of hypoglycaemia in the postnatal period (especially when breastfeeding), and advise them to have a meal or snack available before or during feeds. [2008]
410
1.6.3Women who have been diagnosed with gestational diabetes should stop blood glucose‑lowering therapy immediately after birth. [2008]
411
1.6.4Women with pre‑existing type 2 diabetes who are breastfeeding can resume or continue metformin immediately after birth, but should avoid other oral blood glucose‑lowering therapy while breastfeeding.
412
413

1.6.5Women with diabetes who are breastfeeding should continue to avoid any medicines for their diabetes complications that were stopped for safety reasons when they started planning the pregnancy. [2008]

424
425

427

Terms used in this guideline
453
Disabling hypoglycaemia
454
Repeated and unpredicted hypoglycaemia, requiring third‑party assistance, that results in continuing anxiety about recurrence and is associated with significant adverse effect on quality of life.
455
HbA1c levels
456
HbA1c values are reported in mmol/mol, using the International Federation of Clinical Chemistry and Laboratory Medicine (IFCC) standardised HbA1c test. The equivalent values in %, using the Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT)‑aligned HbA1c test, are reported in parentheses.
457
Level 2 critical care
458
Care for patients who need detailed observation or intervention, including support for a single failing organ system, postoperative care, and patients 'stepping down' from higher levels of care.
