Pregnancy and spinal cord injury (SCI) Key Points
4
Women with lesions above T6 and T10 need special consideration (see below)
5
All women with SCI are at high risk of pressure ulcers, anaemia and UTI
6
Rate of stillbirths and congenital malformation is not increased when conceiving after spinal cord injury
7
Pregnancy limits mobility further in 4.5% of women with SCI
8
If SCI occurred at a young age, or was associated with pelvic trauma, clinical pelvimetry should be performed
9
Vaginal birth is the usual mode of delivery in women with SCI
10

Risks with lesions above T6
12
Bradycardia
13
Hypotension
14
Autonomic dysreflexia (AD) - hypertension, bradycardia, headache, flushing
15
Respiratory muscles may be affected
16
Spirometry checked if vital capacity (VC) <12-15 ml/kg then mechanical ventilation offered
17
Susceptible to autonomic dysreflexia (AD) and spasms (see below)
18
A rise in systolic blood pressure (SBP) of 20-40 mmHg above baseline is considered a sign of AD
19
20
Risks with lesions above T10
21
Perception of fetal movements is altered
22
Perception of uterine contractions is altered
23
Higher frequency of breech or transverse lie, especially in lesions above T10
24

Autonomic dysreflexia
26
Refers to an uninhibited sympathetic nervous system response. As the spinal cord injury separates the parasympathetic (craniosacral division of ANS) from the sympathetic branch (thoracolumbar chain) the negative feedback loop is affected. The body is unable to restore autonomic equilibrium
27
Most common trigger is bladder or bowel distension.
28
29
Spasms
30
12% incidence of worsening of spasticity in pregnancy
31
Treatment of spasms in pregnancy is with baclofen (intrathecal via pump preferred to oral as risk of neonatal withdrawal symptoms with oral baclofen)
32
Oxybutinin used for the control of bladder spasms in pregnancy
